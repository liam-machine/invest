**Date:** February 28, 2026
**Direction:** Long (event-driven catalyst)
**Theme:** Companies likely to announce Block-style AI-driven workforce reductions, triggering a positive stock reaction
**Time Horizon:** Short-term (1–6 months)
**Geography:** US-listed companies

---

## The Block Playbook — What Just Happened

On February 26, 2026, Block (XYZ) announced it was cutting ~4,000 of its ~10,000 employees — nearly half its workforce. CEO Jack Dorsey framed the cuts as an AI-driven transformation, not a sign of business distress. The results:

- **Stock reaction:** +24% after-hours, +17% next-day trading
- **Guidance raised:** 2026 gross profit growth guidance increased from 17% to 18%; adjusted operating income target of $3.2B at 26% margin
- **New efficiency target:** "$2M+ gross profit per person" — 4x pre-COVID levels
- **Dorsey's prediction:** "Most companies will have made large headcount reductions due to AI adoption within a year"

**Why the market loved it:** Block proved that a healthy, growing business could operate with half the people by leveraging AI. The cuts weren't a sign of weakness — they were a signal that management was serious about margin expansion and capital efficiency.

---

## The Pattern — How to Identify the Next Block

To screen for the next company to make a similar move, I looked for these characteristics:

| Signal | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **COVID-era overhiring** | Companies that 2x+ headcount during 2020–2022 likely have significant fat to cut |
| **Healthy/growing revenue** | Cuts from a position of strength get rewarded; cuts from weakness get punished (see C3.ai's -20%) |
| **AI tools already deployed internally** | Companies already using AI for support, code, operations can credibly frame cuts as AI-driven |
| **Low revenue per employee vs. peers** | The clearest signal of overstaffing — more room for efficiency gains |
| **Management has signaled willingness** | CEO commentary about AI efficiency, hiring freezes, or prior small rounds of cuts |
| **Stock underperforming** | Compressed valuation means a restructuring catalyst has more upside potential |

## Idea Cards

### 1. Intuit (INTU) — Highest Conviction — "The Turbo-Charged Turnaround"

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Market Cap | ~$115B | Down from $200B+ peak |
| Headcount | ~17,000 | After 1,800 cuts in mid-2024 |
| Revenue/Employee | ~$970K | Reasonable but room to improve |
| Stock YTD 2026 | **-45%** | Severely beaten down |
| Revenue Growth | ~15% | Healthy, driven by TurboTax + QuickBooks |

**Why Intuit is the most likely "next Block":**

- **Already started cutting:** Intuit laid off 1,800 in mid-2024, explicitly citing AI. CEO Sasan Goodarzi described it as "reallocating investment toward critical strategic areas." This is the same playbook Block used — just smaller scale so far
- **Rumored 18% further cut:** Unconfirmed reports suggest Intuit may be planning a much larger restructuring, which would affect ~3,000+ employees
- **AI product already launched:** Intuit Assist (GenAI financial assistant) is being embedded across TurboTax, QuickBooks, Credit Karma, and Mailchimp. The product rationale for fewer humans is already in place
- **Proprietary data moat:** Intuit has what analysts call "Financial DNA" — decades of tax, accounting, and small business financial data that no competitor can replicate. AI actually *strengthens* this moat because the models improve with more proprietary data
- **Stock is crushed:** Down 45% in 2026 amid the "SaaSpocalypse." A Block-style restructuring announcement could be the catalyst to reverse the decline
- **Dorsey practically named them:** When Dorsey said "most companies will make large headcount reductions within a year," the fintech and SaaS sectors — where Intuit sits — are ground zero

**Key Risks:**
- If cuts are paired with weak guidance (like C3.ai), the stock goes lower, not higher
- The "SaaSpocalypse" may have legs — Intuit's seat-based revenue could face structural headwinds if AI reduces the number of small businesses that need accountants
- Management may choose gradual attrition over a dramatic Block-style announcement

**Catalyst timeline:** Next earnings report or potential standalone restructuring announcement within 1–3 months

### 2. Salesforce (CRM) — "4,000 Agents Replace 4,000 Agents"

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Market Cap | ~$275B | Down from $350B+ peak |
| Headcount | ~70,000+ | Still bloated post-COVID |
| Revenue/Employee | ~$590K | Below Intuit, above Workday |
| Stock YTD 2026 | ~-15% | Underperforming |
| Revenue Growth | ~11% | Decelerating but stable |

**Why Salesforce is a strong candidate:**

- **Agentforce is the smoking gun:** Salesforce's AI agent platform has 4,000+ paid customers and $100M ARR. CEO Marc Benioff has been explicit that Agentforce is replacing customer support roles — this is the exact mechanism Block used
- **Still 70,000+ employees:** Salesforce acknowledged post-COVID overhiring and has done prior rounds of cuts (7,000+ in 2023). But at 70,000+, there's substantial room for another Block-style reduction
- **Revenue per employee of ~$590K is low:** For a software company with 76%+ gross margins, having 70,000+ employees producing ~$590K each signals significant bloat. Compare to Atlassian at $500K+ with far fewer employees and a self-serve model
- **Benioff is signaling:** His Q4 2026 earnings call was dominated by Agentforce commentary. The narrative is being set — "AI agents will do the work of humans, and we'll monetize per conversation rather than per seat"
- **Activist pressure:** Starboard Value and other activists have pushed for margin expansion. A large headcount cut would be directly responsive to their demands

**Key Risks:**
- Salesforce is a massive company — a Block-style 50% cut is unrealistic. More likely 10-20% (~7,000-14,000 jobs), which may not generate the same shock-and-awe stock reaction
- If Agentforce cannibalizes traditional CRM seats faster than it replaces revenue, the math doesn't work
- Benioff may prefer the "quiet attrition" approach over a headline-grabbing announcement

**Catalyst timeline:** Q1 FY2027 earnings (May 2026) or standalone restructuring announcement

### 3. Workday (WDAY) — "The PEPM Death Spiral Escape"

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Market Cap | ~$55B | Down from $90B+ |
| Headcount | ~18,800 | Significant for a mid-cap SaaS |
| Revenue/Employee | ~$420K | Low for enterprise SaaS |
| Stock YTD 2026 | **-39%** | Massive decline |
| Revenue Growth | ~15% | Solid but market isn't rewarding it |

**Why Workday fits the pattern:**

- **Existential AI threat creates urgency:** Workday's core business — Human Capital Management software priced per-employee-per-month — is directly threatened by AI reducing enterprise headcounts. If companies need fewer humans, they need fewer HCM seats. This creates existential pressure to act boldly
- **Revenue per employee of ~$420K is low:** For an enterprise SaaS company with 75%+ gross margins, this signals meaningful overstaffing
- **Stock down 39% creates desperation:** When a stock drops this much, management faces enormous pressure to do something dramatic. A restructuring announcement would signal "we get it, we're adapting"
- **AI pivot underway but not yet rewarded:** CEO Aneel Bhusri is pushing AI agents for HR workflows ("Chapter 4"), but the market hasn't given credit. A headcount reduction would prove the AI tools actually work — Workday would be eating its own cooking

**Key Risks:**
- The irony of an HCM company cutting its own workforce is hard to manage narratively
- If the cuts signal that even Workday can't grow in a world where AI reduces headcounts, it could reinforce the bear thesis rather than counter it
- Customer churn risk if enterprises interpret Workday's own cuts as confirmation that HCM software is a dying category

**Catalyst timeline:** Next 1–3 months, potentially tied to a strategic pivot announcement

### 4. PayPal (PYPL) — "The Quiet Restructurer"

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Market Cap | ~$65B | Down from $350B peak (2021) |
| Headcount | ~24,400 | Down 21% from peak but still high |
| Revenue/Employee | ~$1.3M | Higher than peers but margin pressure |
| Stock 52-week | ~flat | Range-bound, waiting for catalyst |
| Revenue Growth | ~8% | Slow but stable |

**Why PayPal could pull a Block move:**

- **Already in the middle of a multi-year restructuring:** PayPal has cut 21% of its workforce over 4 years and allocated $300M to technology restructuring. The infrastructure for a larger cut is in place
- **AI fraud detection is the rationale:** PayPal's AI-driven fraud detection and transaction automation have reduced manual review costs while improving accuracy. This provides the same "AI made these jobs redundant" narrative that Block used
- **CEO Alex Chriss is a "new broom":** Chriss took over in late 2023 with a mandate to streamline operations. He's made targeted cuts but hasn't yet done a Block-style dramatic move
- **Block is a direct competitor:** If Block can operate Cash App with half the people, PayPal's board will be asking "why can't we?" The competitive pressure to match Block's efficiency is immediate
- **Payments is the right sector:** Both Block and PayPal process payments, serve merchants, and run consumer apps. If AI can automate 50% of Block, it can likely automate 20-30% of PayPal

**Key Risks:**
- PayPal's slower revenue growth (8% vs Block's 24% gross profit growth) means cuts could be interpreted as desperation rather than strength
- The stock has already re-rated from $350 to $65 — some of the "efficiency story" may be priced in
- PayPal's workforce is more global and more regulated (financial services compliance) than Block's, limiting how aggressively they can cut

**Catalyst timeline:** Q1 2026 earnings or standalone announcement within 2–4 months

### 5. Shopify (SHOP) — "Already Walking the Walk"

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Market Cap | ~$130B | Large-cap e-commerce |
| Headcount | ~8,100 | Down from ~12,000+ peak |
| Revenue/Employee | ~$1.1M | Efficient after prior cuts |
| Stock YTD 2026 | ~-10% | Modest decline |
| Revenue Growth | ~25% | Strong |

**Why Shopify is a candidate:**

- **CEO Lütke's memo was the Block playbook before Block:** In April 2025, Lütke told all employees that before requesting new hires, they must prove AI can't do the job. This is exactly the philosophy Block later executed on
- **Already cut 33% since 2022:** Shopify has gone from 12,000+ to 8,100 employees while growing revenue 25%. They've proven the model works
- **The next cut would be smaller but still impactful:** With 8,100 employees, even a 15-20% cut (~1,200-1,600 jobs) would signal continued commitment to AI-first operations

**Key Risks:**
- Shopify has already done the heavy lifting — the incremental catalyst from further cuts may be smaller
- Revenue growth is strong enough that the stock may not need a restructuring catalyst
- Shopify competes for AI talent; too-aggressive cuts could hurt their AI capabilities

**Catalyst timeline:** Possible at any earnings report, but less urgent given strong revenue growth

## What to Monitor

To catch these announcements before they happen (or position ahead of them), watch for:

1. **Earnings calls mentioning "efficiency," "operating leverage," or "AI-driven productivity"** — these are the precursor phrases before cuts are announced
2. **Hiring freezes or "AI-first hiring" mandates** — Shopify's memo was a 10-month lead indicator
3. **Restructuring charges appearing in financial statements** — companies often take charges 1-2 quarters before the actual layoffs
4. **Activist investor filings** — activists explicitly push for headcount reduction to improve margins
5. **Competitor announcements** — Block's move creates pressure on PayPal, Stripe (private), Adyen, and other fintechs to respond

---

## Important Caveats

1. **Not all AI-driven cuts get rewarded.** C3.ai cut 26% of its workforce and the stock dropped 20% because it was paired with weak revenue guidance. The market rewards cuts from strength, not cuts from desperation
2. **Timing is uncertain.** These are event-driven catalysts without fixed dates. Positioning requires patience or options strategies
3. **Ethical considerations.** This screen is analyzing market mechanics, not endorsing mass layoffs. The human cost of these decisions is real and significant
4. **This is not investment advice.** This is a thematic screening exercise. Every idea requires additional fundamental research before any investment decision
